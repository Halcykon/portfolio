Improving Upstream Data Analysis

Restructuring how geoscientists analyze data

 
 

Company
ExxonMobil

Team
Upstream IT

Time
6 months

Role
Research Lead

 
 

CHALLENGE

Improve the petrophysicists’ workflow

Petrophysicists currently sift through through terabytes of noisy data to make sense of how the underground looks like.

The process is a little like herding cats in the dark - just when you think you’ve got them all sorted out, a new one appears out of nowhere and all the cats are loose again.

(Except here, the cats are drilling reports.)

SOLUTION

Streamline data preparation

My interviews all pointed to the massive pain of data preparation. A significant portion of Petrophysicist work is spent on low-value tasks (ex. finding, compiling, and correcting data). I selected this scope based on its high impact and feasibility.

INITIAL EXPLORATION

What is the data “life-cycle”?

I wanted to explore how data flowed. From raw measurements to final reports, what happens to the data? Who touches it? How are data shaped?

We categorized our questions into 4 main categories:

People

Who touches it? Who do they receive data from, and who do they send data to?

Pain Points

How seamless are the baton passes from one user to the next?

What frustrations do the users encounter at each step?

Priorities

What are the potential opportunities, from “quick wins” to “aspirational/transformative”?

Processes

How is data transformed? Why is it done this way?

Environment: Where is data being generated and analyzed? Does the environment (ex. on-premise) constrain data processing?

Time span: How does timing play a role in data generation and analysis? Does data processing follow a coherent sequence?

USER INTERVIEWS

We interviewed 1 senior and 2 junior petrophysicists.

Based on their interviews, we created a flowchart of their current work process and developed persona cards.

I created these pocket personas as handy reference guides for our team (proprietary information redacted)

KEY INTERVIEW FINDINGS

Prevent Simple Errors
Catching simple data errors early (unit conversion errors, mislabeled data) can save thousands of hours of analysis. Alert petrophysicists early so they can quickly request data revisions.

Display where data is in the pipeline
Petrophysicists have no visibility whether data is in transit, tied up in legal, or non-existent. Allow petrophysicists to check the availability of data before they start analysis.

Surface raw data
A key function of petrophysicists is to distrust data; they want to validate analysis by checking the original measurements. Allow them to easily check if raw data exists.

Keep relevant data grouped
Relevant data are often scattered across various vendors, some taking 6+ months to receive. Create a more intuitive system for grouping files.


SURPRISING INSIGHT

Encourage Petrophysicists to distrust data

We originally believed our solution would focus on removing data prep work from petrophysicists, giving them more bandwidth on business-critical work.

However, our interviews surfaced the petrophysicists’ need to validate data before analysis. It was important for them to see how the sausage was made — otherwise they couldn’t trust the sausage.

We pivoted from automating to enhancing data preparation. If Petrophysicists need to be involved from the beginning, how might we make data prep easier for petrophysicists?

CO-DESIGN SESSION

I organized a co-design workshop with a senior petrophysicist.

We sketched out interface mock-ups and storyboards. I asked him to walk me through previous projects as a launching point for ideation.

 

INTERFACE DESIGN

User tests helped us clarify ambiguous features.

We conducted user tests with 2 proxy users (geoscientists) and 1 senior petrophysicist.
In our usability tests, our goal focused on reducing the users’ cognitive load during data preparation.

Our key metrics were:

  1. Are all of our users' data concerns actively surfaced by the system?

  2. Do users find it easy and intuitive to find the datasets they needed?

  3. Did users easily grasp what each UI feature did? (i.e. colors/icons/buttons/layout placement is unambiguous to their task)

 

OUTCOME

Our insights helped drive and prioritize product requirements.

Because the product team was still tackling issues connecting various databases, building the end-user interfaces was far down the product backlog. But we took our research lessons with us and influenced product development.

  1. Counter to leadership assumptions, we proved the importance of keeping raw data in the Petrophycisist workflow. Our data-driven approach helped the product team save development cycles.

  2. Our insights on reducing cognitive workload influenced near-term product requirements. Specifically, how users preferred to access files, what types of error alerts could be pushed to the user, and how to surface data pipeline visibility.

LINGERING QUESTIONS…

I enjoyed the complexity of the problem space and the systems-thinking required to tackle this challenge. If I were to continue this assignment, I would have loved to explore these questions.

  • In situations where raw data is non-existent, what signals could serve as proxy measures for trust?

  • Users say they want all the data yet also say they are overwhelmed by the data. At what point do we design for what they really want, not what they think they want?

  • Would it be better to build this as a dashboard or an “all-in-one” geoscientist product?
