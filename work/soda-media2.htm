Soda Media Insights

Deriving insights on how social media feels about Coke and Pepsi

 
 

Project Description

Using Machine Learning to analyze online user sentiment

Teammates
Asha Toulmin

Time
1 month

Role
Co-developed research approach; Refined code for insights

 
 

CHALLENGE

Does Social Media like Coke or Pepsi?

We analyzed Twitter, Instagram, and Reddit posts about Coke and Pepsi to determine which brand performs better, what events influence brand perception, and recommend potential opportunities. We looked at data from September 1st to October 30th, 2017.

CONCLUSION

Short Answer: Coke.

But also:

Coke should go into the Weight Loss market

Pepsi should partner with luxury brands

INITIAL EXPLORATION

We prioritized original content (instead of reposts and metadata)

Our dataset contained worldwide social media content referencing Coke and Pepsi from Twitter, Instagram, and Reddit. We prioritized content rather than post metadata (gender, location, etc), so we focused only on posted content to provide as much raw data as possible. 

Given that this is a text-based analysis, we chose to use NLP as our analysis tool over other algorithms. 

RESEARCH GOALS

  • Present how the average user felt about Coke and Pepsi and the intensity of that feeling

  • Recommend opportunities that would benefit each company

From this, we derived more specific goals.

  • Determine which brand holds the greater amount of positive sentiment

  • Determine what drives positive/negative sentiment for both brands

  • Uncover words associated with both brands

  • Provide suggestions to improve overall business for both brands

EXPLORATORY DATA ANALYSIS

Normalizing our data from bot posts

We tested 10% of the data initially. Even with a subset of our data, we found that bot posts significantly skewed our results. 

For example,  we found a constantly occurring “chemtrail” conspiracy post that clogged the top 100 frequently occurring Pepsi words with irrelevant words like "Apocalypse”, “Virgin Mary”, “Dog Fat”, etc:

"Nanochips are sprayed by the gov’t using chemtrails... Dog fat and placenta is in McDonalds, Pepsi, toothpaste, antiaging, anticancer, vaccines, perfume, etc.; that’s why you should not be using anything that modern society has to offer..."

- Weird bot post associated with our Pepsi data

It was quickly apparent that frequently spammed content was our largest issue. We wanted to make sure that our end results would be built on solid research, so we decided that genuine sentiment would come from uniquely written posts, and refined our code to delete any duplicate data.

We prepped the data through the following ways:

  • Removed retweets or reshares: We removed any content that was categorized as a "retweet" data type

  • Removed duplicate content: For uncategorized similar posts, we ran a boolean check to remove duplicate values

  • Removed capitalization: Capitalization does not matter here, so we changed all text to lowercase

  • Standardized punctuation: We ensured consistency among punctuation (e.g. different quotation types: “” vs "" )

  • Removed null values

(note: Data cleaning/refinement occurred throughout the course of the project as we discovered other issues)

DATA EXPLORATION

We analyzed Coca-Cola and Pepsi data distinctly, so we created two separate datasets based on whether the posted content included Pepsi or Coca-Cola related phrases.

Sentiment exploration: We used TextBlob (a Python library) to analyze each row of unstructured text. The model returned two pieces of data:

Polarity: The overall (positive/negative) sentiment expressed in a text corpus
Subjectivity: The calculation of personal opinion in a text corpus (for this project, higher is better)

We then plotted the distribution of polarity scores to understand if there were any key differences in overall sentiment spread.

Both brands, on average, scored a slightly positive sentiment. Since the medians do not deviate too far from the means, we expected a normal distribution. However, it is always good to check with some data visualization!


From these findings, it was clear that:

1.  Coke enjoyed slightly more positive sentiment
2.  Both brands followed a normal distribution curve, with a slight skew towards positive sentiment.

Data Modeling: Hashtag Sentiment Drivers

With an overall idea of the sentiment distribution for our Coca-Cola and Pepsi data, we began to explore the underlying factors driving the overall trends.

Hashtag analysis: Using the SpaCy library, we extracted the 100 most frequently used hashtags, and then further separated the list into brand and relevant non-brand mentions.

Coca Cola: Histogram Overview

As seen above, #dietcoke and #shareacoke are very popular brand mentions. This reflects a successful product and marketing campaign, respectively.

For relevant non-brand mentions, we observed a weight loss trend around #dietcoke, specifically regarding food.

 

Pepsi: Histogram Overview

As seen above, #crystalpepsi, #pepsimax, and #pepsibattleofthebands are very popular brand mentions. The first two are products (Pepsi Max is analogous to Diet Coke), and Battle of the Bands is a Pakistani TV show (analogous to American Idol).

For relevant non-brand mentions, we observed that Pepsi had a strong association with humor and memes.

Data Modeling: Parts of Speech Sentiment Drivers

We ran Parts of Speech (POS) tagging to extract the top 100 noun, adjective, and verbs for Coca-Cola and Pepsi. We took the most frequently occurring words and sorted them by most positive and negative sentiment.

Greenpeace, despite being classified as positive, actually portrays negative sentiment against soda companies' lack of environmental concern.

  • Topo Chico is a well-known sparkling mineral water brand acquired by Coca-cola.

  • Coca-Cola donated $4MM to Mexico for disaster relief after hurricane Maria.

  • None of the negative sentiment nouns are significant.

[Battle of] the bands appears to be a successful TV show in Pakistan, which also shows up in our top hashtag results.

  • Gatorade is often mentioned in a list of companies to boycott for sponsoring the NFL (for the anthem protests)


(We also ran POS tagging and sentiment sorting on adjectives and verbs. However, since the findings were not significant, we present them in the project file)

CONCLUSION

Which brand performed better on social media?

From September 1st to October 30th 2017, social media users associated more positively with Coca-Cola than with Pepsi. During this time, Pepsi was widely panned for not being sensitive enough to contemporary racial tensions. Discussions of Kendall Jenner's controversial ad revived while the Anthem protests were in full swing (Pepsi is an NFL sponsor).

What sentiments drove each brand?

  • Coke's positive drivers revolve around weight loss, diet coke, their #shareacoke marketing campaign, and their Hurricane Maria relief fund to Mexico.

  • Coke's negative drivers revolve around Greenpeace’s awareness campaign about litter caused by soda companies

  • Pepsi's positive drivers revolve around their TV show battle of the bands, their reintroduction of Crystal Pepsi, and their Pepsi Max soda.

  • Pepsi's negative drivers revolve around being associated with the NFL (when football players knelt during the anthem protests of Black Lives Matter), as well as Kendall Jenner’s Pepsi commercial whom many felt trivialized BLM.

Actionable insights

Besides looking at top hashtags, nouns, verbs and adjectives as drivers of sentiment, we also uncovered interesting discoveries for each brand.

Coca-Cola should explore partnerships with weight loss companies. We found that many #slimmingworld posts trended in September 2017, specifically mentioning the deliciousness of the “diet coke chicken” recipe. Given Coke’s already strong association with the weight loss industry, we recommend that Coke explore potential opportunities here. 

Pepsi should explore partnerships with high-end luxury goods. We found interesting clusters around #rolexpepsi, #watches, and #rolexgmt. It seems that Rolex released a popular Pepsi-themed watch (above). Given the social media fascination with this watch, Pepsi may have an opportunity to capitalize on the luxury goods market.

Technical Limitations

The complexity of language is astounding, and it is amazing to see what NLP tools can do with large text datasets. However, NLP is not yet robust enough to fully understand the intricacies of language. Below, we enumerate some of these examples.

  • False Positives/False Negatives: Sentiment analysis cannot understand sarcasm, intent, or irony. As such, it did not correctly classify that the sample text “Don't you hate on Pepsi, stupid”, was in fact, a positive sentiment towards Pepsi.

  • Posts mentioning both Coke and Pepsi: Some posts mentioned both brands, which may have decreased the sentiment difference between Coke and Pepsi. Running symmetric difference on POS/Hashtags may be a future consideration.

  • Lack of emoji/image analysis: As we trend towards more non-text based posts (Instagram, Snapchat), the lack of robust emoji and image recognition tools severely limit sentiment analysis.

  • Irrelevant posts: Many posts are simply full of irrelevant (automated) hashtags. It is difficult to determine the relevancy of a post from this type of post content.

  • Specific word extraction: #chocolate vs #cocacolaclassic (chocolate would be extracted as a coke-related word)

REFLECTION

I was eager to explore quantitative methods because I felt that qualitative research felt too subjective. Ironically, this project made me more doubtful about big data analysis. I now feel compelled to check how the data was prepped and framed. I've noticed that conclusions were highly dependent on the data source, how it was cleaned, where data scientists focused their analysis, and what algorithms they chose (and how they tweaked it).

More importantly, I learned that algorithms still require human supervision to conclude robust findings. As seen with the above limitations, algorithms are far from perfectly understanding the complexity of communication.

If I were to redo this project again, I would have removed Instagram data, as those posts were void of substantive text content. If I had more time, I would also have loved to implement average sentiment over time by plotting the date on the x-axis.
